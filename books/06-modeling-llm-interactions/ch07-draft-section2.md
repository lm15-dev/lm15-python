## What Dependencies Buy

The argument against dependencies is easy to make because the costs are vivid — version conflicts, security incidents, cold start measurements. The argument for dependencies is harder to make because the benefits are invisible. You don't notice the bug that `pydantic` caught. You don't measure the time saved by not writing an HTTP connection pool. You don't count the security vulnerabilities you didn't introduce by not reimplementing TLS certificate validation.

But the benefits are real, and dismissing them is a form of arrogance that's worth naming.

**Specialization.** `requests` has been maintained for over a decade. Its HTTP implementation handles redirect chains, cookie jars, proxy tunneling, chunked uploads, digest authentication, client certificates, and dozens of edge cases in the HTTP specification that most developers have never encountered. The `requests` maintainers have fixed bugs that you will never discover because your test suite doesn't cover proxy authentication with NTLM over HTTPS through a corporate firewall. When you reimplement HTTP with `urllib`, you reimplement the 5% you need and skip the 95% you don't — until a user deploys your library behind a corporate proxy and discovers that your HTTP layer can't handle it.

The same argument applies at every level. `pydantic` validates nested data structures with custom types, handles JSON Schema generation, provides serialization to and from multiple formats, and catches construction errors with messages precise enough to debug from. Your `__post_init__` validation checks that text parts have text. It doesn't check that the text is valid UTF-8, that the tool call's arguments match the schema, that the image data is actually valid base64, or that the usage numbers are non-negative. These are bugs that exist in the latent space of your type system — possible but uncaught. `pydantic` catches most of them. `__post_init__` catches some.

`httpx` provides async HTTP — an entire concurrency model that `urllib` can't offer at any level of effort. You can't add async to `urllib` with clever coding. You need a different HTTP stack, which means a different transport, which means a different dependency. The zero-dependency choice forecloses async entirely — not as a tradeoff but as a hard exclusion.

**Maturity.** These packages have been tested by millions of users in millions of environments. Their edge cases are documented. Their error messages are refined. Their performance is optimized. Your reimplementation has been tested by you, in your environment, with your test suite. The gap between "works in my tests" and "works in production worldwide" is the gap that dependencies bridge.

**Community and ecosystem.** When a developer uses `requests`, they can search Stack Overflow and find thousands of answered questions. When they use `urllib.request.urlopen` with custom headers and timeout handling, the answers are fewer, more scattered, and often wrong. Dependencies buy not just code but *context* — a community of users who've solved the same problems and documented the solutions.

**The bakery argument.** You could grow your own wheat, mill your own flour, and bake your own bread. The bread might be excellent. But the baker down the street has been baking for twenty years, uses better equipment, and produces consistent results at lower cost. Using the bakery isn't laziness — it's rational specialization. The same logic applies to HTTP libraries, validation frameworks, and async runtimes. They exist because the problem is well-understood, the solution is stable, and the cost of reimplementation exceeds the cost of dependency — for most projects, in most contexts.

The caveat — "for most projects, in most contexts" — is doing important work in that sentence. It's the qualifier that makes the zero-dependency position viable for lm15 specifically, even though it would be wrong for most libraries. The next section examines why.
